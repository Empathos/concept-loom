# Sourdough notes

The starter is livelier since switching to 50/50 rye and white for feeds.
Peaks in five hours at room temperature instead of eight. Rye is rocket
fuel; adjusting feed ratios beats adjusting schedule.

Hydration experiments: 75% is my ceiling with this flour. The 80% batches
taste the same and handle like glue. Chasing high hydration for its own sake
was an internet-induced detour.

Cold retard timing matters more than I thought. Twelve hours in the fridge
gives mild loaves; twenty-four gives the tang people actually comment on.
Thirty-six starts to break down the crumb. The fridge is the flavor dial.

Scoring finally clicked: one confident slash beats four hesitant ones. The
blade angle (shallow, almost parallel to the surface) is what makes the ear,
not the depth.

Batch notes: the Saturday loaf with the overnight autolyse had the best
crumb of the year. Autolyse is free — it costs planning, not effort. Do it
every time.

Oven spring correlates with shaping tension more than with proofing time in
my log. Under-proofed but tightly shaped beats perfectly proofed and slack.
Surface tension is the variable to obsess over.

Baked for the neighbors again. A loaf is the highest-leverage gift I know:
two euros of flour, an afternoon of waiting, and people remember it for
months.

Winter kitchen note: at 19°C ambient, bulk fermentation takes nearly twice
the summer time. The dough doesn't read the recipe. Watch the dough (50%
growth, domed edges, a few surface bubbles), not the clock.
